Conceptual Skills I've Gained

Content Authoring for Reusability
I was unaware and naive about how challening content reuse was. It's a technical communicator's responsibility to ensure that content can be altered, replicated and disseminated easily. This is becoming standard practice. Our course readings and LavaCon presentations demonstrated this well and I almost feel guilty for not considering it beforehand.

A few key takeaways:
  • Organize and link topics to create a coherent web of information. When you write in topics, it’s easier and more productive for multiple writers to contribute to the larger set of information.
  • Write only one procedure per task topic so that you can easily manage, organize and reuse topics and so that your users can find specific tasks when they need them.
  • Before you write task topics, be sure you understand your audience and their goals. Don’t write task topics that focus on how the product works.
  • Use consistent formatting in topics that contain similar information.

Web Design and Layout
Previously, I had only used Wix and Wordpress templates to build my websites. I have tons of experience crafting layouts through Adobe InDesign, particularly for fact sheets and print collateral, but I hadn't applied those skills to a website. The "box model" was familiar to me and I already felt comfortable establishing hierarchies of information, but this was often done without accompanying visual assets like photos or videos. Working with flexible layouts completely changes the game and it's been cool to learn about.

A few key takeaways:
  • Front-load the important information. The journalism model of the “inverted pyramid" starts with the content that is most important to your audience, and then provide additional details.
  • When writing for the web, use plain language that allows users to find what they need, understand what they have found and allows them to use it to meet their needs. It should be actionable, findable and shareable.

User Analysis
Everyone is on the internet nowadays, making it imperative that you understand your unique audience's needs and capabilities. I've gained a better understanding of how to look at content from someone else's perspective, as well as how to recognize my own bias when creating content.

A few key takeaways:
  • Many companies aren’t doing their due diligence of making that content accessible for persons with disabilities. From headlines and form fields, to the use of images and color contact, the majority of websites today are not Web Content Accessibility Guidelines. I've been guilty of this in my own work and it's sad that this wasn't even something I considered before this class. It seems like this should be in every "introduction" course for digital technologies. This is something I should've had in v1 of my personal learning narrative, but unfortunately it wasn't brought to my attention.
  • People are bombarded with information each day and there's increased saturation in the digital world. It's up to technical communicators to maximize their audience's attention and retention.
